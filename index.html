A Captive Audience: FCC Regulation and the Right Not to Listen

How is the right not to listen established and how could FCC regulation reinforce it?

1971

Cohen v. California

Click to expand

Type(s) of Speech at Issue

The Right Not To Listen Obscene Speech Peaceable Assembly in Public Space

Case Overview

In April, 1968 a young protestor named Paul Robert Cohen wore a jacket while visiting the Los Angeles County Courthouse. He had stenciled the phrase “Fuck the Draft” on the back of the jacket. He was arrested upon leaving the courthouse and was charged and convicted of malicious and willful disturbing of the peace. Cohen appealed his conviction and the case made its way up to The Supreme Court.

The Court ultimately found in favor of Cohen and overturned his conviction. In the majority decision the justices established what would become the backbone of the captive audience doctrine still used in First Amendment law today.

Boundary that The Court draws

With Cohen The Court established a test for limitation on free expression that included the following criteria: expression can be limited when it is a) obscene, b) directed at any one person in an aggressive manner, or c) directed at a captive audience whose right to privacy is being infringed upon by being forced to listen to the expression. Because Cohen hadn't met any of these criteria with his jacket: he hadn't used “fuck” in an obscene way, he hadn't directed the message on the back of his jacket at any one person, and he had displayed it in a public place where others could reasonably be expected to extricate themselves from viewing the expression, The Court found his conviction unconstitutional on First Amendment grounds.

Consequences of The Court's Boundary

The consequences of the distinction drawn in the case are still felt today in First Amendment law. Among other criteria, the establishment of the captive audience doctrine shaped not only freedom of speech case law but also privacy law going forward. A captive audience is one who cannot reasonably be expected to “escape” the circumstances under which they are hearing a given expression or whose right to privacy is being violated by the speaker. This portion of the criteria set forth by The Court holds particular significance to FCC regulation as broadcast media enters the privacy of the home by public means, inherently creating a potentially captive audience in the form of anyone who turns on a television set or a radio.

2011

Snyder v. Phelps

Click to expand

Type(s) of Speech at Issue

Peaceable Assembly in Public Space The Right Not To Listen

Case Overview

Westboro Baptist Church made a reputation for itself by using inflammatory and offensive language in its protests. These protests took place in the early 2000s and included signs with messages like “God Hates Fags” and other homophobic hateful language. When the Iraq war started they began protesting soldiers' funerals with signs and chants that held a similar hateful tone. One father of the fallen soldier felt that they didn’t have the right to protest funerals and sued Fred Phelps, the leader of the church for emotional distress.

The case made its way to the Supreme Court who ultimately decided that Westboro wasn’t liable for their speech, a decision owing in large part to the captive audience doctrine.

Boundary that The Court draws

Because Westboro's speech was done in a public space and they packed up their signs and removed their protest shortly into the private part of the funeral and stayed away during the burial the Court saw no captivity in the audience inside the church. As such, holding to earlier opinions that reinforced the captive audience doctrine set forth in Cohen v. California, The Court upheld that the ideological content of the speech in question doesn't constitute emotional distress.

Consequences of The Court's Boundary

Westboro's speech wasn't seen as causing material emotional distress during the private portion of the ceremony where the captive audience doctrine could theoretically apply.

If, say, the protestors had remained all the way through the official proceeding portion of the funeral then they would have had a captive audience to their message. In this scenario, Westboro could have their speech acted on by those whose right not to listen to their message was being infringed on by having to choose between either remaining in the church and hearing Westboro's protest or leaving the private funeral to escape it.

1978

FCC v. Pacifica Foundation

Click to expand

Type(s) of Speech at Issue

The Right Not To Listen Obscene Speech

Case Overview

In October, 1973 a New York radio station that was part of the Pacifica radio network broadcast a set by the comedian George Carlin called “Filthy Words”. A man listening to the broadcast filed a complaint with the Federal Communications Commission claiming that he was concerned about the ease with which a child could have come across the broadcast. After the complaint, the Commission issued an order clarifying what constituted obscene language and that it wasn't permissible to be broadcast on public airwaves during daylight hours.

Pacifica appealed the order and the case came before The Supreme Court. The Court found in favor of the Commission, reinforcing the right of private individuals to govern what is broadcast in the privacy of their own homes.

Boundary that The Court draws

The Court draws a line at the front door of any private residence or property under its jurisdiction. Within the bounds of private property people are allowed to govern themselves and what they and their dependents listen to. Owing to the public ownership of the airwaves the FCC has a duty to reinforce that right and ensure that what's broadcast into the home doesn't infringe upon the right of the individual to consume what they want to and not accidentally be captive to a broadcast they don't want to be.

Consequences of The Court's Boundary

Pacifica's consequences are still felt today, the 1975 Declaratory Order still stands as the watchword of obscenity on the airwaves. Moreover, The Court’s decision reinforces the duty of the FCC to safeguard the right of American broadcast listeners and viewers to govern what's broadcast in their homes.

The Right To Change The Channel

The jurisdiction of the Federal Communications Commission is the broadcast spectrum on the public airwaves. According to your own website the FCC “regulates interstate and international communications by radio, television, wire, satellite and cable in all 50 states, the District of Columbia and U.S. territories.” (What We Do, n.d.) Americans who use broadcast television to receive their local news are particularly subject to FCC regulations. A part of FCC jurisdiction is who to lease frequencies on the airwaves to and what those entities transmit over those frequencies to American citizens.

The FCC is responsible for managing and licensing the electromagnetic spectrum for commercial users and for non-commercial users including: state, county and local governments. This includes public safety, commercial and non-commercial fixed and mobile wireless services, broadcast television and radio, satellite and other services. In licensing the spectrum, the Commission promotes efficient and reliable access to the spectrum for a variety of innovative uses as well as promotes public safety and emergency response. (Licensing, n.d.)

As demonstrated by the 3 Supreme Court cases included in this infographic, Americans have the right not only to freedom of expression under First Amendment law but also to freedom from expression. First codified in the limitation criteria described in the majority opinion in Cohen v. California Americans include what has come to be known as the captive audience doctrine.

The ability of government, consonant with the Constitution, to shut off discourse solely to protect others from hearing it is, in other words, dependent upon a showing that substantial privacy interests are being invaded in an essentially intolerable manner. (Cohen V. California, 403 U.S. 15 (1971), n.d., p. 21)

Essentially, American citizens have the right to be held captive to anyone’s expression, doing so constitutes a violation of privacy of the captive citizen.

This right not to be captive to expression was reinforced to include their television sets in the privacy of their own home in FCC v. Pacifica Foundation. As Justice John Paul Stevens noted in the majority opinion

[T]he broadcast media have established a uniquely pervasive presence in the lives of all Americans. Patently offensive, indecent material presented over the airwaves confronts the citizen not only in public, but also in the privacy of the home, where the individual's right to be left alone plainly outweighs the First Amendment rights of an intruder. (FCC V. Pacifica Foundation, 438 U.S. 748 (1978), n.d.)

In outlining the right to be left alone in the privacy of the home Justice Stevens draws from the captive audience doctrine described as part of the limitation criteria set forth in Cohen v. California. Elsewhere the Court has reinforced this right as a matter of degree of captivity. “[T]he court has also held that captive audience doctrine applies ‘where the degree of captivity makes it impractical for the unwilling viewer… to avoid exposure.’” (“The Captive Audience Doctrine Today,” 2025, p. 3)

As recently as 2011 the Court reaffirmed its commitment to the doctrine in Snyder v. Phelps. Westboro Baptist Church prevailed in the emotional distress case brought against them by the father of a fallen soldier whose funeral Westboro was picketing. However distasteful and vile the act of picketing outside of a funeral is, the Court held firm to their First Amendment right in part owing to the lack of captivity of their audience.

The majority ruled that the First Amendment protected the church from liability for their speech on a matter of public concern in a public place, which did not disrupt or physically interfere with a funeral. (Rosenberg, 2021, p. 169)

As the funeral started Westboro packed up their protest and left, ensuring that they were not holding the funeral goers captive to their expression.

The role of the FCC to vouchsafe that right is enshrined in the spirit of your own 1975 declaratory order that was the very subject of the Pacifica case. The order calls out the following in section 9

Broadcasting requires special treatment because of 4 important considerations: … (2) radio receivers are in the home, a place where people’s privacy interest is entitled to extra defence (“Citizen’s Complaint Against Pacifica Foundation Station WBAI (FM), New York, NY -- Declaratory Order,” 2018, p. 4)

Echoing the privacy interest of captive audience doctrine enforcement the FCC’s order safeguards listeners and viewers in their own homes from becoming a captive to any one entity or broadcaster.

However, there are a grand total of 4 broadcasters as of 2021 according to a Harvard University Study serving all American local cable markets. Some markets are only served by one of these broadcasters. When an American consumer in one of these under-diversified markets wants to change the channel to see another viewpoint, say on their local cable news, they are not able to escape the views of one of these broadcasters. A First Amendment attorney might see this as the FCC allowing the creation of a captive audience to the one corporate broadcaster in a given market.

The consumers in these underserved markets are unable to reasonably find escape from the viewpoints of the media conglomerate that’s serving their local broadcast communities.

In light of this exploration and the case law supporting it I call on the administrators at the Federal Communications Commission to regulate their allocation of frequencies to allow for more competition in local broadcasting markets. You are failing in your duty to the First Amendment rights of American citizens in not regulating against this media monopoly. As a citizen of the United States I ask you to please do better and start inquiries into breaking up this monolith of corporate media ownership.
